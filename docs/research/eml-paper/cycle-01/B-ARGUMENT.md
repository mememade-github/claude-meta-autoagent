# Minimal Generating Basis for Elementary Calculator Functions

## 0. Statement of the problem

The primitive set P₀ of the scientific calculator is:

| Class | Members |
|-------|---------|
| Constants | π, e, i, −1, 1, 2 |
| Unary | exp, ln, reciprocal, sqrt, square, negate, halve, logistic, sin, cos, tan, arcsin, arccos, arctan, sinh, cosh, tanh, arsinh, arcosh, artanh |
| Binary | +, −, ×, ÷, log (arbitrary base), pow, mean, hypot |
| Inputs | x, y (free variables, not counted as primitives) |

We view each primitive as a symbol with an arity and a standard interpretation over ℂ (with principal branches for multi-valued functions). A **basis** B ⊆ P₀ generates P₀ if every primitive p ∈ P₀ can be written as a finite tree whose nodes are labelled by elements of B and whose leaves are labelled by elements of B or by the input variables x, y.

We seek the minimum |B|, and an explicit basis achieving it.

---

## 1. Motivation

### 1.1 Reductions in other domains

Several classical examples show that large primitive catalogs collapse to tiny generating sets:

- **Boolean logic.** From the 16 binary Boolean functions, NAND alone generates every n-ary Boolean function. The reduction is not a conservation of "essential" operations but an observation that logical AND and NOT can both be expressed as compositions of NAND with itself, and that AND+NOT generates the rest.

- **Combinators.** The combinators S and K generate every λ-term. An even more extreme one-combinator basis (iota) exists. The trick is that application — the only binary operation available — is rich enough to simulate abstraction.

- **Peano arithmetic.** The natural numbers and all their arithmetic emerge from a constant 0 and the successor function S. Addition is a primitive-recursive definition on top. Multiplication, exponentiation, and every arithmetic primitive are induced.

- **Fields and groups.** A field ℚ(α) over ℚ is generated by a single element α plus the field axioms. For ℂ over ℚ we need both i (a single algebraic element) and the whole topological closure; but for the *algebraic* closure 𝐐̄ of ℚ we need only the four field operations plus root extraction.

- **Univariate + addition for continuous multivariate functions.** A classical result in real analysis: every continuous function of several real variables decomposes into superpositions of continuous univariate maps and the binary operation of addition. (One sketches this via iterated interpolation on a product grid, approximating each coordinate projection by univariate steps; the limit object remains continuous under suitable parameterizations.) The point is that addition is a surprisingly powerful binary combiner when richly many unary maps are available.

### 1.2 Why we should expect a small basis here

The calculator catalog has obvious redundancies visible to anyone who has seen an introductory complex-analysis course:

1. Trigonometric and hyperbolic functions are real and imaginary translates of the complex exponential. The identity e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ — which one can derive by comparing the power series of the left and right sides term-by-term — is not an accident; it is the *definition* of cos and sin once the exponential has been extended to ℂ.
2. All inverse trigonometric/hyperbolic functions have closed-form expressions in ln with ratios and square roots as arguments (because the inverse of exp is ln, and the inverses of the exp-built trig and hyperbolic functions invert algebraically to ln of algebraic expressions).
3. All powers and roots are exp-of-ln rescalings.
4. All binary arithmetic is either multiplicative (×, ÷, pow) or additive (+, −); under exp the multiplicative group of ℂ* becomes the additive group of ℂ.

These observations suggest that **two transcendental operations (exp, ln), one binary, and one complex constant** might be enough. We will confirm this by a direct construction and argue that nothing smaller works.

### 1.3 A guiding diagram

```
            exp
   (ℂ, +)  ───►  (ℂ*, ·)
           ◄───
            ln
```

Over ℂ, exp is a surjective group homomorphism from the additive to the multiplicative group, with kernel 2πiℤ. ln is its (branched) section. Everything multiplicative collapses into everything additive once we cross this bridge. Our basis needs the bridge (exp, ln), the additive group's binary operation (+), and a single complex element to populate the constants — that element is i.

## 2. The reduction procedure

### 2.1 Formal rules

Given a candidate basis B ⊆ P₀:

- (R1) **Definitional elimination.** If p ∈ B admits a closed-form expression p = t(q₁, …, qₖ) with t a tree whose leaves are in B ∪ {x, y} ∪ (B \ {p}) (i.e. referring only to other members of the basis or inputs), then p is redundant and may be removed.
- (R2) **Constant-from-constant reduction.** If constant c can be built by operations in B from other constants in B, then c may be dropped.
- (R3) **Variable preservation.** Inputs x, y are always available at leaves; they are not counted in |B|.

We apply R1 and R2 repeatedly until no further elimination is possible; the remaining set is a *fixed point* of our reduction procedure.

### 2.2 What "closed-form" means

A closed-form reduction is a finite tree. No limits, no series, no integrals. Each internal node is labelled by an operation of the appropriate arity; each leaf is a constant of the basis or a free variable.

### 2.3 Branch cut convention

Each multi-valued operation (ln, non-integer pow, sqrt, inverse trig, inverse hyp) is fixed to its principal branch: for ln, the branch with imaginary part in (−π, π]; for sqrt, the one with non-negative real part or positive imaginary part; etc.

The reductions we exhibit are **formal identities over ℂ away from branch cuts**. At isolated branch-cut crossings, different principal-branch choices can disagree by additive multiples of 2πi (for ln-based identities) or by sign (for sqrt-based identities). We treat this as a known limitation; it is a property of the primitive catalog itself, not of our reduction.

### 2.4 Order of elimination

We eliminate in four passes, chosen so that each pass strictly reduces the surviving basis:

1. **Derived constants** (π, e, 2) expressed from simpler ones.
2. **Derived unary primitives** (trig, hyperbolic, their inverses, powers, logistic, negate, halve, reciprocal, sqrt, square) expressed in terms of {exp, ln, +, ×} and constants.
3. **Derived binary primitives** (−, ÷, pow, log, mean, hypot) expressed in the same terms.
4. **Cross-category reductions** exploiting x·y = exp(ln x + ln y) and i² = −1 to remove × and eliminate the −1 constant.

## 3. Progressive reductions

Write Pₖ for the basis after stage k. We track the count |Pₖ| of non-variable symbols.

### Stage 0 — full catalog

```
P₀  =  {π, e, i, −1, 1, 2}               (constants, 6)
     ∪ {exp, ln, reciprocal, sqrt, square, negate, halve, logistic,
        sin, cos, tan, arcsin, arccos, arctan,
        sinh, cosh, tanh, arsinh, arcosh, artanh}  (unary, 20)
     ∪ {+, −, ×, ÷, log, pow, mean, hypot}         (binary, 8)
```

|P₀| = 34.

### Stage 1 — eliminate derived constants

- 2 = 1 + 1.
- e = exp(1).
- π = −i · ln(−1). (Principal branch: ln(−1) = iπ, so −i · iπ = π.)

These reductions leave {i, −1, 1} as the surviving constants.

```
P₁  =  {i, −1, 1} ∪ (unary20) ∪ (binary8)          |P₁| = 31
```

### Stage 2 — eliminate trig and hyperbolic via exp

Using the complex-exponential representations:

- sinh(x) = (eˣ − e⁻ˣ) / 2
- cosh(x) = (eˣ + e⁻ˣ) / 2
- tanh(x) = sinh(x) / cosh(x)
- sin(x)  = (eⁱˣ − e⁻ⁱˣ) / (2i)
- cos(x)  = (eⁱˣ + e⁻ⁱˣ) / 2
- tan(x)  = sin(x) / cos(x)

All six right-hand sides use only {exp, +, −, ×, ÷, i, 2}, so they are composable in our surviving primitives.

```
P₂  =  P₁ \ {sinh, cosh, tanh, sin, cos, tan}       |P₂| = 25
```

### Stage 3 — eliminate inverses via ln

- arsinh(x) = ln(x + √(x²+1))
- arcosh(x) = ln(x + √(x²−1))
- artanh(x) = ½ ln((1+x)/(1−x))
- arctan(x) = −(i/2) · ln((i−x)/(i+x))   (equivalently (i/2)·ln((i+x)/(i−x)), or (1/(2i))·ln((1+ix)/(1−ix)))
- arcsin(x) = −i · ln(ix + √(1−x²))
- arccos(x) = −i · ln(x + i·√(1−x²))

Each right-hand side uses {ln, +, −, ×, ÷, sqrt, square, i, 1, 2}. Good.

```
P₃  =  P₂ \ {arsinh, arcosh, artanh, arcsin, arccos, arctan}    |P₃| = 19
```

### Stage 4 — eliminate the remaining unary primitives

- reciprocal(x) = 1/x = exp(−ln x)
- sqrt(x) = exp((ln x)/2)
- square(x) = exp(2 ln x)                  (equivalently x·x once × is expressed)
- negate(x) = (−1) · x                     (a pure multiplication by the constant −1)
- halve(x) = x/2
- logistic(x) = 1 / (1 + exp(−x))

All expressible in {exp, ln, +, −, ×, ÷, i, −1, 1, 2}.

```
P₄  =  {i, −1, 1, 2} ∪ {exp, ln} ∪ {+, −, ×, ÷, log, pow, mean, hypot}
```

|P₄| = 14.

### Stage 5 — eliminate derived binary primitives

- pow(x, y) = exp(y · ln x)
- log_x(y) = ln(y) / ln(x)
- mean(x, y) = (x + y) / 2
- hypot(x, y) = exp((ln(x·x + y·y))/2)

Each side uses {exp, ln, +, −, ×, ÷, 2}.

```
P₅  =  {i, −1, 1, 2} ∪ {exp, ln} ∪ {+, −, ×, ÷}          |P₅| = 10
```

### Stage 6 — collapse subtraction and division into + and ×

- −(a, b) = a + (−1)·b
- ÷(a, b) = a · exp(−ln(b)) = a · reciprocal(b), which in P₅'s remaining primitives is a × exp(−1 · ln b).

Needs × and −1; ok.

```
P₆  =  {i, −1, 1, 2} ∪ {exp, ln} ∪ {+, ×}                |P₆| = 8
```

### Stage 7 — eliminate × via exp and ln

The keystone identity:

  **x · y  =  exp( ln(x) + ln(y) )**

is the inverse image of the additive group structure under exp, valid away from branch cuts.

```
P₇  =  {i, −1, 1, 2} ∪ {exp, ln} ∪ {+}                   |P₇| = 7
```

### Stage 8 — bootstrap the remaining constants from i

- −1 = i · i = exp(ln(i) + ln(i)).
- 1 = (−1) · (−1) = exp(ln(−1) + ln(−1)).
- 2 = 1 + 1.

With i in hand, every integer follows by addition, and every dyadic rational by exp/ln reciprocals. So −1, 1, 2 are all redundant.

```
P₈  =  {i} ∪ {exp, ln} ∪ {+}                              |P₈| = 4
```

This is our terminal configuration.

## 4. Minimal configuration

**M  =  { + ,  exp ,  ln ,  i }**

- `+` : ℂ × ℂ → ℂ, addition.
- `exp` : ℂ → ℂ, complex exponential e^z.
- `ln` : ℂ* → ℂ, principal complex logarithm (Im ∈ (−π, π]).
- `i`  : the constant √(−1), i.e. the distinguished generator of ℂ over ℝ.

**Size** |M| = 4.

### 4.1 Alternate bases of the same size

The reduction procedure is not canonical. Equally minimal bases include:

- `{ −, exp, ln, 1 }` — subtraction reaches −1 from 1, from which i = exp(ln(−1)/2) = exp( ½ ln(exp(... )) ) is reachable; subtraction also yields +.
- `{ ×, exp, ln, i }` — addition is derivable as x + y = ln(exp(x) · exp(y)). Dual to M.
- `{ +, exp, ln, −1 }` — i = exp(ln(−1)/2) gives i; otherwise identical.

Each contains an additive *or* multiplicative binary bridged through {exp, ln} to the other, plus a complex/negative seed constant. None of them is strictly smaller.

### 4.2 Why we pick M

Of the four-element bases, M is the cleanest for the required worked examples: the trigonometric reductions are naturally expressed with +, exp, and i, and no auxiliary bootstrap is needed to produce the imaginary unit.

### 4.3 A subtler three-element basis: variable-cancellation tricks

A careful reader will notice that the four-element minimum depends on our *counting* of constants as atomic basis elements. If we adopt a strictly *functional* view — where any closed-form expression (including a term with variables whose dependence cancels out) represents a function — then the minimum drops to three:

**M′  =  { − ,  exp ,  ln }**

This basis has no explicit constants. All constants are produced by variable-cancellation expressions:

- 0 = x − x (identically zero regardless of the value of x)
- 1 = exp(x − x)
- −1 = 1 − 1 − 1 (three copies of the `exp(x − x)` subtree, chained as `((1 − 1) − 1)`)
- Integers of either sign via iterated subtraction
- i = exp(ln(−1) / 2), where ÷2 is a × with 1/2 and × reduces via exp/ln/+
- + : a + b = a − (0 − b) where 0 = x − x

Every primitive in P₀ then reduces to M′ by the same chain we gave for M. The seven-stage reduction of Section 3 compresses by one stage (skipping the "elimination of the last explicit constant" step, because we never had one).

The catch: this construction uses variable leaves to manufacture constant values. The tree for `sin(x)` then has some leaves labelled `x` (the real input) and other leaves labelled `x` (embedded in the constant-producing subterms); both must receive the same input value, but the constant-producing subterms are independent of that value by construction. Formally, this is a 1-ary expression whose value is the constant function, not a 0-ary primitive. Whether this counts as a "basis element" depends on one's convention: in universal algebra it is perfectly ordinary, in the classical calculator-button view it is a syntactic trick.

We present M (|M| = 4) as the *constant-explicit* minimum and M′ (|M′| = 3) as the *functional-equivalence* minimum. Both are valid answers to the original question under different reasonable conventions.

## 5. Verification strategy

### 5.1 What we must check

For every p ∈ P₀ we must exhibit a closed-form tree Tₚ over M (and the free variables) whose interpretation as a complex function equals p.

### 5.2 Method: an elimination chain, composed

The progressive reduction above is *exactly* such a chain: for each p we have an explicit identity reducing it to earlier primitives, and each of those in turn has an explicit reduction. Composing the chain yields Tₚ for every p.

### 5.3 Three checks for each candidate reduction

For each p we apply three consistency tests:

1. **Syntactic closure.** Every symbol occurring in Tₚ is in M ∪ {x, y}. (Mechanical.)
2. **Functional value at a non-degenerate point.** Evaluate both p and Tₚ at a test point where all branches are unambiguous; verify equality.
3. **Functional value on a dense subset.** Repeat at three or more test points covering distinct branches (real positive, real negative, pure imaginary, generic complex).

These three together do not *prove* equality for all inputs — two meromorphic functions can agree on any finite set without being equal — but they catch almost every symbolic error and branch-convention inconsistency.

### 5.4 Completeness check over the catalog

We compile a single table listing every primitive in P₀ with its reduction. If the table has an entry for every member of P₀, the basis M generates P₀.

| Primitive | Reduction (composed of M only)                                                   |
|-----------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 1         | exp( ln(i) + ln(i) + ln(i) + ln(i) )                                              |
| −1        | exp( ln(i) + ln(i) )                                                              |
| 2         | (1) + (1)  — i.e. add two copies of the preceding line                           |
| e         | exp(1)                                                                           |
| π         | ×( negate(i), ln(−1) )  where × and negate are themselves decomposed             |
| exp       | identity                                                                         |

Every row's right-hand side uses only M (after full recursive expansion) plus x, y. This completes the verification strategy.

## 6. Worked examples

We pick three primitives of progressively higher complexity and exhibit the full reduction to M. In the formulas below we use a few notational shortcuts that we then expand step by step:

- `MUL(a, b) := exp( ln(a) + ln(b) )` — multiplication.
- `NEG(a) := MUL(MUL(i, i), a)` — since i·i = −1 and −1·a = −a.
- `SUB(a, b) := a + NEG(b)`.
- `DIV(a, b) := MUL(a, exp(NEG(ln(b))))`.
- `TWOI := i + i`.

Each of these is a pure tree over M with i as the sole constant leaf.

### 6.1 Example A: multiplication

`x · y` = `exp( ln(x) + ln(y) )`

This is already in M. The identity holds because exp is a group homomorphism from (ℂ, +) to (ℂ*, ·) — verifiable from the power-series definition exp(z) = Σ zⁿ/n!, which gives exp(a+b) = exp(a)·exp(b) by binomial rearrangement — and ln is its (principal-branch) inverse.

**Check at x = 2, y = 3.** exp(ln 2 + ln 3) = exp(ln 6) = 6 = 2·3. ✓

### 6.2 Example B: sine

Start from the reduction formula

    sin(x)  =  ( exp(ix) − exp(−ix) ) / (2i).

Rewrite without − and ÷:

    sin(x)  =  DIV( SUB( exp( MUL(i,x) ) , exp( NEG( MUL(i,x) ) ) ) , TWOI ).

Now expand each shortcut:

```
MUL(i, x)            =  exp( ln(i) + ln(x) )
NEG(MUL(i,x))        =  exp( ln( exp(ln(i)+ln(i)) ) + ln( exp(ln(i)+ln(x)) ) )
SUB(a, b)            =  a + exp( ln(exp(ln(i)+ln(i))) + ln(b) )
TWOI                  =  i + i
DIV(numer, TWOI)     =  exp( ln(numer) + exp( ln( exp(ln(i)+ln(i)) ) + ln(ln(TWOI)) ) )
```

Folding these in, sin(x) becomes a single tree whose symbols are exactly `+, exp, ln, i, x`. The tree is large — several dozen nodes — but every node is in M.

**Check at x = π/6.** Target sin(π/6) = 1/2. With principal branches ln(i) = iπ/2:

- MUL(i, π/6) = exp(ln i + ln(π/6)) = exp(iπ/2 + ln(π/6)) = i · (π/6) = iπ/6.
- exp(iπ/6) = cos(π/6) + i sin(π/6) = (√3/2) + i/2.
- exp(−iπ/6) = (√3/2) − i/2.
- Difference = i.
- Divide by 2i: i / (2i) = 1/2. ✓

(Note π/6 is itself an M-expression: take π from Stage 1 and halve twice; this is a meta-check of the chain's self-consistency — we did not have to produce π to *verify* sin, only to *evaluate* it.)

### 6.3 Example C: hypotenuse

`hypot(x, y)` = `sqrt( x² + y² )`.

Chain of reductions:

```
square(a)   = MUL(a, a)
sqrt(z)     = exp( DIV( ln(z) , 1+1 ) )
hypot(x,y)  = sqrt( square(x) + square(y) )
            = exp( DIV( ln( MUL(x,x) + MUL(y,y) ) , 1+1 ) )
```

Fully expanded:

```
hypot(x,y) = exp( exp( ln( ln( exp(ln(x)+ln(x)) + exp(ln(y)+ln(y)) ) )
                        + NEG( ln( 1 + 1 ) ) ) )
```

where `1` unfolds to `exp(ln(i)+ln(i)+ln(i)+ln(i))` and NEG unfolds as above. All symbols are in M.

**Check at x = 3, y = 4.** 3² + 4² = 25. sqrt(25) = 5. Via the reduction: MUL(3,3) = exp(ln 3 + ln 3) = exp(2 ln 3) = 9. MUL(4,4) = 16. Sum = 25. ln(25) = ln 25. DIV(ln 25, 2) = (ln 25)/2. exp((ln 25)/2) = √25 = 5. ✓

### 6.4 Example D (bonus): arctangent

Starting from the standard identity arctan(z) = (1/(2i)) · ln((1+iz)/(1−iz)), rewrite 1/(2i) = −i/2 and (1+ix)/(1−ix) = (i−x)/(i+x) (multiply top and bottom by i); this gives

    arctan(x)  =  −(i/2) · ln( (i − x) / (i + x) ).

In M-only form:

```
arctan(x)  =  MUL( NEG( DIV(i, 1+1) ) , ln( DIV( SUB(i,x) , i + x ) ) )
```

Every symbol expands into M.

**Check at x = 1.** Target arctan(1) = π/4. On principal branches: (i−1)/(i+1) = i, so ln((i−1)/(i+1)) = iπ/2. Then −(i/2) · iπ/2 = π/4. ✓

## 7. Why the lower bound is 4 (strict) or 3 (functional) — and not less

The minimum size depends on the interpretation of "constant" in the basis. Under either interpretation we give a matching lower bound and show it is tight.

### 7.0 Which interpretation applies

- **Strict interpretation.** A basis may contain 0-ary primitives (constants) or operators of arity ≥ 1. A term's arity is the number of its free variables. Reconstructing a 0-ary primitive in P₀ (e.g. 1, i, π) requires a 0-ary term. Under this convention, Section 7.1–7.4 below gives |B| ≥ 4.
- **Functional interpretation.** A P₀ primitive p is "reconstructed" iff there is a closed-form term t (of arity equal to p's arity or greater, with extra arguments being functionally ignored) whose interpretation equals the interpretation of p. Under this convention, |B| ≥ 3 as we show in 7.6 below.

Both interpretations are defensible. We argue both and are explicit about which claims rely on which.

### 7.1 Claim 1: B must contain at least one binary operation

Every basis element is either a constant, a unary operation, or a binary operation. If B has no binary operation, then every closed-form tree over B has the shape

    op_n ( op_{n−1} ( … op_1 ( leaf ) … ) )

where each op is unary. Such a tree computes a function of a *single* input — whichever variable sits at the leaf. But `+` is a genuine two-variable function: +(x, y) depends on both x and y. No purely unary tree can represent it. Hence B must contain ≥ 1 binary operation.

### 7.2 Claim 2: B must contain an exp-type primitive

Define the *exp-free class* as the set of complex functions built from compositions of {+, −, ×, ÷, ln, algebraic roots, constants} — no exp, no trig, no hyperbolic, no logistic. We show this class does not contain exp.

Growth-at-∞ argument. Every function f in the exp-free class has growth bounded by a polynomial times a power of log|z| as |z| → ∞ along any ray. (Polynomials grow polynomially; sums, products, and quotients preserve bounded polynomial growth; algebraic roots preserve it up to rational exponents; ln grows like log|z|; compositions of all these produce poly(|z|)·(log|z|)^k-type bounds.) In contrast, exp(z) grows like e^{Re(z)}, which exceeds any polynomial-times-log-power bound as Re(z) → +∞. So exp ∉ exp-free class.

Therefore B cannot consist entirely of exp-free primitives: it must contain at least one primitive whose growth at ∞ is super-polynomial. Among P₀, the super-polynomial primitives are exp, sin, cos, tan, sinh, cosh, tanh, logistic, and pow (when the base or exponent allows). Each of these embeds an exp call, so each can serve the same role modulo a derivation. We take exp as the canonical representative. B must contain one of these, and without loss of generality it is exp.

### 7.3 Claim 3: B must contain an ln-type primitive

Define the *ln-free class*: compositions of {+, −, ×, ÷, exp, algebraic roots, constants} — no ln, no inverse trig, no inverse hyperbolic, no log_x.

Ramification-order argument. Every function in the ln-free class has at most *finite* monodromy around any point: analytically continuing such a function around a small loop returns it to itself after finitely many circuits (the bound depends on the algebraic roots involved). This is because: polynomials, exp, and algebraic roots all have this property (polynomials: none; exp: none; algebraic: finite order equal to the ramification index), and the class is closed under +, −, ×, ÷, and composition — each of which preserves finite monodromy order.

In contrast, ln has *infinite* monodromy around 0: continuing ln around the unit circle once adds 2πi; continuing n times adds 2nπi. So ln is not a function on a finite-sheeted Riemann surface — it requires an infinite-sheeted cover.

Therefore ln ∉ ln-free class, and B must contain a primitive with infinite monodromy somewhere. Among P₀, such primitives are ln, log_x (which is ln(y)/ln(x), reducing to ln), and the inverse trig/hyperbolic functions (which each have infinite monodromy around their branch points, since they all reduce to ln compositions). We take ln as the canonical representative. B must contain one of these, and without loss of generality it is ln.

### 7.4 Claim 4: B must contain at least one constant that "breaks the positive real line"

Consider a candidate basis B containing only non-negative real constants (or no constants at all, with inputs x, y taken to range over positive reals). The operations +, ×, exp, ln, pow, sqrt, mean, hypot all map positive reals to positive reals. So every tree over such a B, with positive real inputs, produces a positive real output.

But the primitive `negate` produces −x, which is negative (or complex) for positive x. This output lies outside the image. So negate is not generated. Similarly, `i` (the constant) is imaginary, not in the image. And `ln` applied to a positive real produces a real — so without a negative or complex constant, even ln cannot give us a non-real value.

Hence B must contain a constant c that is either negative, or has nonzero imaginary part, or else a binary operation (like subtraction) that can *produce* such a value from positive real seeds.

**Options in P₀ that satisfy this:** the constants i and −1; or the binary − (which from any constant c gives us 0 = c − c and then −c = 0 − c).

### 7.5 Putting it together

Claims 1–4 are pairwise independent: no single element of P₀ satisfies two of them simultaneously. Specifically:

- A binary operation cannot also be a unary transcendental (different arity).
- A unary transcendental cannot also be a constant (different arity).
- exp cannot be ln (they are not equal; Claims 2 and 3 require both).
- The "ℂ-entry" role is disjoint from the binary arithmetic role *except* when the binary is − (which supplies its own entry to ℂ from 1 via 1−1−1 = −1 and then ln).

In every case, four roles must be covered by distinct primitives. Therefore under the strict interpretation |B| ≥ 4.

The minimal value is achieved by M = {+, exp, ln, i}; equivalently {−, exp, ln, 1} fuses the binary and ℂ-entry roles but still uses 4 symbols because it needs 1 as a seed under the strict interpretation.

**Conclusion (strict).** |B| = 4 is tight.

### 7.6 Functional-interpretation lower bound: |B| ≥ 3

Under the functional interpretation, variable cancellation (e.g. x − x = 0, exp(x − x) = 1) lets us synthesize constants *without* an atomic-constant basis element. Claims 1, 2, 3 still apply:

- **Binary.** We still need a binary operation to express two-variable primitives like +.
- **Exp-type.** Super-polynomial growth at ∞ still must come from a primitive (exp, sin, cos, etc.).
- **Ln-type.** Infinite monodromy still must come from a primitive (ln or an inverse trig/hyp).

So under either interpretation, |B| ≥ 3. Claim 4 *dissolves* under the functional interpretation: if the binary operation is − or ÷, it self-supplies a "cancellation constant" (x − x = 0, x ÷ x = 1), which in turn seeds access to all needed constants when combined with exp and ln.

Concretely, {−, exp, ln} achieves the lower bound of 3: the binary (−) also produces the cancellation constant 0, from which 1 = exp(0), −1 = 1 − 1 − 1, and i = exp(ln(−1)/2) all follow.

**Two-element bases fail.** Any two-element B ⊆ {binary ops, unary ops} is missing at least one of the three capability roles (binary, exp-type, ln-type), so cannot generate P₀. So |B| ≥ 3 is tight under the functional interpretation.

**Conclusion (functional).** |B| = 3 is tight, achieved by M′ = {−, exp, ln}.

## 8. Open questions and limitations

### 8.1 Branch-cut and zero-pole subtleties

Our identities x·y = exp(ln x + ln y), pow(x,y) = exp(y ln x), and the inverse-trig/hyp expansions are equalities of *multi-valued functions* (Riemann surfaces), but only *partial* equalities of principal-branch functions. Two distinct failure modes:

1. **Branch mismatch by 2πi.** ln(z·w) = ln z + ln w can fail by 2πi when arg(z) + arg(w) leaves (−π, π]. A complete "strict" reduction over principal branches would need additional branch-repair machinery (e.g., modular reduction by 2πi).

2. **Removable singularities at 0.** The identity a·b = exp(ln a + ln b) is undefined when either argument is zero, since ln(0) = −∞. As a limit along a · b → 0 the identity extends continuously to 0, but as a closed-form expression it is only valid on ℂ* × ℂ*. A calculator built on M would need to handle the zero-argument case separately — which is not a closed-form operation in our basis.

Both phenomena are features of the original primitive catalog (which ships principal-branch functions and accepts 0 as a valid multiplicand), not defects of M. A strict minimality statement therefore reads: M generates every primitive in P₀ as a *principal-branch function on the open dense subset of ℂⁿ where all intermediate subexpressions are defined*.

### 8.2 Real-only minimal basis

If we disallow complex constants and restrict inputs to reals, the collapse of trig into complex exp is unavailable. Likely candidates for a minimal real basis are {+, exp, ln, negate, sin} or {+, −, ×, exp, ln, sin} — with sin as an irreducible *real* transcendental primitive whose existence must be postulated. A careful minimality proof in the real-only setting would require showing sin is not expressible as a composition of {+, −, ×, ÷, exp, ln, constants} over ℝ. The underlying idea of such an impossibility proof: the differential field ℝ(x) closed under algebraic extensions, exp, and ln does not contain sin as an element — one can show this by arguing that adjoining sin introduces a genuinely new type of algebraic dependency (sin² + cos² = 1) that does not arise from closures under exp or ln alone. Over reals, sin lacks the complex-exponential shortcut, so this obstruction is real; over ℂ, sin has the exp shortcut and is derivable. We expect the real minimum to be ≥ 5.

### 8.3 Computational cost vs. expressive economy

The reductions blow up expression size substantially. Computing `sin(x)` via M requires ~20–40 exp/ln evaluations, each of which is a Taylor-series call. A calculator built on M would be correct but numerically terrible: repeated ln/exp amplifies rounding error, and near branch cuts accuracy is catastrophic. Minimality of basis is orthogonal to efficiency of implementation.

### 8.4 Alternative transcendental unifications

One can ask whether a *single* unary transcendental function f could replace both exp and ln. Candidates include: the function W satisfying W(z)·e^{W(z)} = z (a unary self-referential exponential-inverse); a unary f that grows factorially and interpolates between integer and complex orders (a gamma-like function); and doubly-periodic meromorphic functions on ℂ (elliptic-function-like). Over ℂ, no single unary primitive of this kind is known to generate both exp and ln by finite composition with + and constants. A negative result here would strengthen our lower bound from "|B| ≥ 4" to "|B| ≥ 4 with a specific transcendental pair required," but we do not prove it here.

### 8.5 Dependence on i as the "chosen" complex constant

We pick i, but any single non-real complex constant c would work (with perhaps longer reductions). The real number −1 is also a valid "entry to ℂ" via ln(−1) = iπ. The reduction from arbitrary c to canonical M-expressions is a small exercise in algebra, but a uniform proof that *every* non-real c suffices (rather than just i and −1) would tie the basis choice to the field-theoretic statement ℂ = ℝ(c) for any c ∉ ℝ.

### 8.6 Extension to more than two variables

The construction scales: any n-ary primitive g(x₁, …, xₙ) that is elementary in the classical sense — meaning it lies in the differential-field closure of ℂ(x₁, …, xₙ) under the operations of +, −, ×, ÷, exp, ln, and algebraic roots — remains reducible to M by the same rules. M is a generator not just for P₀ but for the full class of elementary functions of any finite number of variables over ℂ.

### 8.7 A category-theoretic formulation

Roles Claims 1–4 encode could be formalized as follows: the functor sending a basis B to the "function clone" it generates factors through (a) a binary-clone component, (b) a pair of mutually-inverse transcendental generators, (c) a pointed complex base point. Proving |B| = 4 is minimal amounts to proving this factorization is essentially unique. We sketch the idea but do not formalize it.

## 9. Summary

Starting from a 34-symbol scientific-calculator primitive set, we reduced step-by-step to two minimal generating bases, each minimal under a different reasonable convention:

> **M  =  { + ,  exp ,  ln ,  i }**   — size 4, with explicit constants.
>
> **M′ =  { − ,  exp ,  ln }**         — size 3, synthesizing constants from variable cancellation (`x − x = 0`).

The reduction relied on three keystone identities:

- **Multiplicative-to-additive bridge:** x·y = exp(ln x + ln y) collapses × into {+, exp, ln}, because exp is a homomorphism from (ℂ, +) to (ℂ*, ·).
- **Complex exponential:** trig and hyperbolic functions are (linear combinations of) exp(ix) and exp(−ix), which one sees by comparing power series.
- **Principal ln of negatives:** ln(−1) = iπ seeds all trigonometric constants.

Lower bounds. Under the *strict* interpretation (0-ary constants must appear as atomic basis elements), |B| ≥ 4 is forced by four independent role-constraints: a binary operation, an exp-type primitive (super-polynomial growth at ∞), an ln-type primitive (infinite monodromy), and an explicit complex-or-negative constant. Under the *functional* interpretation (constants may be manifested by variable-cancelling terms), |B| ≥ 3 is forced by the first three constraints alone — the binary's cancellation identity (x − x or x ÷ x) supplies the constant seed.

Caveats. Reductions are exact up to branch-cut conventions and zero-argument edge cases. For principal-branch-strict equality on isolated rays, and for the multiplication-via-exp/ln identity at 0, small 2πi corrections or separate-case handling may be needed; these are limitations of the primitive catalog, not of the bases.
